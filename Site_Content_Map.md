### Section 3.2: Operating Principles (5-6 blocks)

**What goes here:**
Short sections (3-5 sentences each) covering how you approach the core problems that CoS, Ops Directors, and TPMs face. Use a title + short paragraph format.

**Principle 1: "Start with visibility, not process"**
Write about how your first move in any new function is to create a single source of truth — a dashboard, a digest, a weekly report — so leadership can see what's actually happening before you start changing things. Reference the executive Product Digest you built.

**Principle 2: "Build the team around the problem, not the org chart"**
Write about how you scaled from 6 to 75+ not by following a hiring plan, but by solving the next problem and building the team that problem required. How you became the talent pipeline because you were closest to the work.

**Principle 3: "Tooling should serve workflows, not the other way around"**
Write about your experience transitioning across three support platforms and standardizing Jira/Notion. The lesson: don't pick a tool first, define the workflow first, then find the tool that fits.

**Principle 4: "Make decisions with models, not gut feel"**
Write about building the budget and headcount forecasting models in Google Sheets + Apps Script. How you gave leadership data to make hiring decisions instead of guessing. How you think about instrumenting decisions.

**Principle 5: "Distributed teams need intentional culture"**
Write about managing 75+ people across LATAM, Europe, and South Africa. What you learned about async communication, accountability across time zones, and building culture when you can't be in the same room.

**Principle 6: "Use AI as a force multiplier, not a replacement"**
Write about how you've used AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor, Claude Code, Apps Script + AI) to move faster — writing, analysis, automation, coding. Frame it as an operating philosophy: the best operators adopt tools early and teach others by showing what's possible.

**Guidance:**
- Each principle should be grounded in a real example from your experience
- Don't make these abstract management platitudes — make them specific
- This page is essentially your "interview answers" written out ahead of time
- A CoS hiring manager reading this should think "this is exactly how I want my operator to think"
- Keep each block to 3-5 sentences. Don't write essays.

# PAGE 6: CASE STUDY #3 — Bringing AI Into the Workplace

**URL:** `/case-studies/ai-in-the-workplace`
**Goal:** Position yourself as an operator who *uses* AI as a practical tool — not an AI engineer, but someone who understands how to integrate AI into workflows, evaluate tools, and drive adoption through action rather than mandates. This is the piece that makes AI-native companies interested in you for ops/CoS roles.

---

### Section 6.1: Header

- Title: "Bringing AI Into the Workplace"
- Subtitle: "A practitioner's approach to AI adoption through action, not policy"
- Key themes strip: "Workflow automation | AI-assisted development | Tool evaluation | Lead-by-example adoption"

---

### Section 6.2: The Context

**Write 2-3 paragraphs covering:**
- The broader moment: AI tools became widely available in 2023-2024, and most companies were figuring out what to do with them
- Your situation: you weren't an engineer or data scientist, you were an ops leader — but you saw the opportunity to move faster
- Your approach: instead of waiting for a company AI strategy or policy, you started using tools yourself and showed others what was possible

**Guidance:**
- Frame this as a leadership story, not a technical story
- The angle is: "The best way to drive AI adoption is to demonstrate value, not write memos about it"

---

### Section 6.3: How I Used AI (4 workstreams)

**This section should have 4 distinct subsections, each 2-3 paragraphs:**

**Workstream 1: Writing, Analysis, and Decision Support**
- What tools you used (Claude, ChatGPT)
- Specific use cases: drafting executive communications, summarizing long documents, analyzing data, preparing for meetings, synthesizing information across sources
- What changed: speed, quality, or both?
- Be specific: "I used Claude to [do X], which cut the time for [Y] from [hours] to [minutes]"

**Workstream 2: Workflow Automation**
- What tools you used (Zapier + AI, Apps Script + AI, etc.)
- What you automated: reporting pipelines? ticket triage? data consolidation?
- Give a concrete example of a workflow you built
- What was the before/after? What did this free you up to do instead?

**Workstream 3: AI-Assisted Development**
- What tools you used (Cursor, Claude Code, Copilot)
- What you built: internal tools, dashboards, scripts, automations
- Frame this as: "I'm not a developer, but AI tools let me build things that would have required engineering resources"
- Be honest about your skill level — "beginner-intermediate Python" is fine. The story is that AI tools enabled you to punch above your weight.

**Workstream 4: Tool Evaluation and Team Adoption**
- How you evaluated AI tools for the company (what did you look at? what criteria?)
- How you shared what you learned with colleagues
- The lead-by-example approach: what did other people start doing after seeing your results?
- Any resistance you encountered and how you handled it

**Guidance:**
- Every workstream needs at least one concrete example with a before/after
- Don't oversell — you're not claiming to be an AI expert. You're claiming to be an operator who adopted AI early and effectively.
- The "AI-assisted development" section is particularly compelling for CoS roles at tech companies — it shows you can prototype and build without needing engineering support
- This section should be 600-800 words total across all four workstreams

### Section 6.4: What I Learned About AI Adoption

**Write 3-5 short paragraphs or principles, such as:**

- "The operators who adopt AI earliest will have the biggest advantage" — this is a skill gap that's only widening
- "Most AI ROI comes from boring use cases" — not chatbots or generative art, but summarization, drafting, data analysis, and automation

**Guidance:**
- Write these as genuine lessons, not LinkedIn platitudes
- Ground each one in something specific you experienced
- This section positions you as someone who *thinks* about AI adoption strategically, not just someone who uses ChatGPT

# WRITING TIPS

- **Word count targets:** Case studies should be 1,200-1,800 words each. About page under 500 words. How I Operate under 800 words. Home page under 150 words.
- **Voice:** First person, confident but not arrogant. Write like you're explaining to a smart peer, not presenting to a board.
- **Numbers:** Include them everywhere. Revenue, team size, time saved, percentage improvements. Numbers are what make operational case studies credible.
- **Honesty:** Mention what was hard, what you'd do differently, what didn't work. This is what separates senior operators from people padding their resume.
- **Formatting:** Use subheadings, bold key phrases, and keep paragraphs to 3-4 sentences. Hiring managers skim before they read.
- **The "so what" test:** After every section, ask yourself: "Does this prove I can do the job I'm applying for?" If not, cut it or reframe it.
